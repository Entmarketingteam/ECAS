Christopher,

*The Kill Chain* maps the problem precisely: speed of the decision loop is the decisive variable. Sense, Understand, Decide, Act — whoever closes that loop faster wins.

I've been thinking about how the same logic applies to commercial B2G procurement, specifically in the DFR market.

The municipal procurement cycle has its own kill chain. An agency gets a COPS grant. A department head researches vendors. A shortlist forms. An RFP gets written around the vendors already in the room. By the time the notice hits SAM.gov, the evaluation is effectively over.

Most drone vendors enter at the RFP stage — they're acting after the loop has already closed.

We built a system called ContractMotion that maps the pre-RFP window: COPS grant awards, FAA BVLOS waiver filings, city council DFR resolutions, ShotSpotter contract renewals. These signals precede the RFP by 60–180 days. They indicate commitment, not intent — an agency that has filed a BVLOS waiver and received a COPS Technology grant is not researching. They're buying.

The parallel to your thesis: the vendor who gets into the evaluation window first sets the RFP requirements. That's not a sales advantage — it's a structural one. The decision loop has already run. Vendors who show up at the SAM.gov notice are fighting a battle that was decided upstream.

I realize this is a much smaller theater than what you're writing about. But the mechanism is the same, and I thought you'd appreciate the application.
